Part 1 · Defining the Foundation

What does “village” mean to you? How would you define or describe “the village”?

Village is a community of connections: a network of mutually supportive relationships, role models for our children, and shared rituals like meals, tea, fireside chats, dancing, play, and co-creating spaces for healing, movement, exploration, and art. It feels like family-style support and stewardship of the land.

A village is a way of life that feels closer to how we were meant to live. Traveling created that feeling of connection, whereas parenting young babies felt insular. As we move through uncertain times, building the village now is an essential act of sustainability and survival.

Beyond that urgency, the dream is to build a dance community and become a hub for playful creativity. The village hosts sober daytime dance parties, family dance events, and traditions that attract open-minded people alongside a sustainable food forest.

What “ingredients” of your village are important to you?

Dancing and music, children and elders together, homeschooling and alternative learning, creativity, open-mindedness, playfulness, presence, low-drama community, motivation, self-responsibility, family, health, movement, healing, sustainability, integrity, and generous gift economies.

Values that ground the community

  • Creativity, open-mindedness, and playfulness
  • Presence, family, and community care
  • Stewardship, sustainability, and truthful communication
  • Motivation, self-responsibility, generosity, and gift exchange

Location, roles, and governance considerations

The village is rooted here and close by. Because we own the space, we want to clarify roles and how caretaking or residency agreements are held. Governance should align with shared vision while honoring responsibility for the land.

Who are the people, roles, and contributions you imagine?

  • Crafty and handy collaborators who help build and maintain the property
  • Artists, performers, musicians, and teachers
  • Grandparents, elders, and actual close family members
  • Friends who bring ideas to life and help things get done

Specific people you want to include

  • Sisters, mom, and extended family
  • Inviting dad to be more involved
  • Daniel’s parents and wider family
  • Friends near and far: Elle, Danny, Rose, and others we attract

How do you deal with conflict or disagreement?

We agree not to handle conflict over text. Conversations happen in person or verbally, with implied agreements clarified upfront. When commitments are important, pair verbal check-ins with written notes so expectations stay clear.

What agreements and commitments unite the village?

We want overarching agreements tied to the shared vision, plus the practical bones of what it means for someone to stay here. These will keep evolving as the village grows.

How do the kids experience the village?

They learn gardening, sustainability, building, music, plays, and endless skills from other children and elders. There are dance parties, art, and random interests that we alone cannot provide.

How does the village provide for them?

The village offers long-term survival, sustainable futures, love, nourishment, and a lifelong love of learning. It fosters empathy, relationship skills, and gives kids time with others while parents collaborate on shared and individual projects. It also grants parents space for partnership and creative work.

How do your kids contribute to the village?

Through love, laughter, curiosity, and learning how to give. The hope is that they grow up understanding how to contribute and participate actively in community.

Current glimpses of the village

Mother’s helpers, nanny Gianna, visiting friends, and extended family calls provide support. NextDoor connections offered meals and companionship postpartum, and friends like Danny, Rose, Elle, and Ali bring shared interests. Past communal living brought joy alongside lessons about agreements and expectations.

Share more about life right now

Postpartum support from neighbors and helpers was nourishing, although coordinating it while caring for newborns proved challenging. FaceTime keeps distant family close, and there is a deep desire to include dad and nearby friends more regularly. The longing is for weekly family time and for loved ones who cherish the children to be present in everyday life.

Who is part of your village right now?

  • Immediate family, even if geographically distant
  • Sisters in Florida, Maine, and Washington, plus mom
  • Friend Elle, friends Danny and Rose in Denver, and neighbor friends
  • Two mother’s helpers, a supportive nanny, and friend Gio (navigating commitments)

What feels missing?

Nearby grandparents, weekly time with family, aunties, uncles, community help with projects, dinners together, shared motivation, dance-loving creatives, and the steady presence of people who care deeply about the children.

What do you want to learn more about or try?

  • Attracting the right people and holding agreements
  • Handling transitions when things don’t work out
  • Nurturing relationships and sustaining community
  • Creating learning and schooling environments
  • Exploring currency systems and gift economies (Daniel’s curiosity)

Part 2 · Day in the Village of Your Dreams

What does a day in the village feel like?

Regular walks with neighbors, people dropping in to help with shared visions, and hands in the garden or dance studio. Community gathers around dance and music, artists collaborate, families share dinners, and the babies have playmates and older role models nearby. The village thrives on magical celebrations and sustainable living.

Moments that surfaced during visioning

Planning projects with others over breakfast, coordinating dance events, dancing with the kids, dad teaching Finn how to build, sisters and friends sharing dinner, and seeing multiple projects actively moving across the property. Teachers flow in and out to support the children’s curiosity.

Imagine that you're waking up in the village of your dreams. Where are you? What do you see and sense?

Morning light pours into a home nestled on the land, surrounded by gardens and the dance barn. Children play nearby while birdsong and distant laughter signal neighbors already stirring. The air smells of breakfast and freshly brewed tea as the family stretches into a new day.

What is the morning ritual like, and how are you nourished?

Breakfast is shared around a lively table with family, visiting aunties, and friends who stayed overnight. Conversation flows from gratitude to music playlists to the day’s projects. The nourishment is as much relational as it is physical—hot meals, warm drinks, and the comfort of community presence.

How do you prepare for the day ahead? What are you looking forward to?

After breakfast everyone collaborates on the day’s intentions: tending the food forest, rehearsing for daytime dance gatherings, or inviting neighbors to help with a new build. There is excitement about joyful work—planning events, practicing music, and welcoming helpers who want to get involved.

What is the pace of your morning? Are you staying or heading out?

The rhythm is unhurried yet focused. Most mornings are spent on the land, opening the dance space or garden for collaborators. Sometimes the family heads to a neighbor’s plot or welcomes a caravan of friends arriving with tools and children in tow.

Describe arriving—whether you are arriving somewhere or others are arriving to you.

Friends arrive laughing, carrying baskets of produce or instruments. Kids splash into the yard and run toward the play areas. Elders park and stroll in, greeting everyone with hugs before settling into the kitchen or workshop spaces. Arrival feels like an embrace.

What are you doing, receiving, or contributing? How do you connect to creativity and purpose?

The day weaves between facilitating dance jam sessions, mapping garden beds, and setting up art projects for kids. Creativity flows through DJ practice, storytelling circles, and collaborative design. Purpose shows up in stewarding the land and holding space for people to express themselves.

Who else is around? What are they doing?

Grandparents share wisdom with the children, craftspeople tackle repairs, teachers host pop-up learning pods, and musicians rehearse for upcoming gatherings. Friends rotate through childcare, cooking, and building projects so everyone can contribute where they shine.

What does lunchtime look like?

Lunch is a potluck spread in the garden or under the shade of the barn. Everyone brings something they love—homegrown veggies, soups, breads—and meals are shared picnic style. Stories and laughter keep the energy bright.

How does the afternoon unfold?

After lunch there’s a soft transition: some take a siesta, others continue working on the barn studio or tending crops. Kids rotate between learning circles, play rehearsals, and building forts with guidance from elders. There is room for rest and for momentum.

What nourishes you personally throughout the day?

Stealing moments for dance practice, grounding barefoot in the soil, sharing tea with a mentor, or journaling in the loft overlooking the land. Music, movement, and meaningful conversations refill the cup.

How does the late afternoon feel?

The pace slows. Families gather to watch kids rehearse a skit while others harvest vegetables for dinner. Sunlight pours through the barn as musicians wind down rehearsals and folks check in on project progress before evening.

Describe dinnertime rituals.

Dinner becomes a ceremony—sometimes outside around a long table, other times in the barn amid twinkling lights. Everyone contributes: cooking, setting the altar, telling stories, or offering gratitude. Daniel often shares tales by the fire, and kids lead a song or dance.

What happens after dinner?

Evenings drift into fire circles, music jams, and quiet conversations beneath the stars. Loose ends—tidying shared spaces, coordinating tomorrow’s helpers—are wrapped up together before families peel off for bedtime.

How do you ready yourself for rest?

Bedtime includes checking in with the children, gratitude sharing, and gentle movement or meditation. Before sleep there is a moment to note what blossomed that day and what delights are on the horizon.

What does the environment look and feel like as you close the day?

The home glows softly, art projects hang to dry, instruments rest beside the hearth, and the scent of herbs lingers in the air. Outside, the land is dotted with cozy gathering spaces—fire pits, garden beds, and play structures built by many hands.

Who are you being throughout this day?

A joyful host, grounded steward, and creative collaborator—someone who moves with generosity, invites play, and stays devoted to the vision of communal flourishing.

Part 3 · Potential Obstacles

What resistance or “yeah, but…” thoughts surfaced?

How many people can we truly hold in our lives? Do we really want potential drama? It feels important to honor personal space even while craving community. Past communal living brought joy—like the stretch when Vic and Gio lived with us—but also highlighted the need for clearer agreements.

There is worry about judgment tied to past experiences online, including Daniel’s history, as well as finding common ground with local neighbors. The hope is that intentional events will attract the right people.

How do you imagine working with these obstacles?

Honoring clear boundaries, naming expectations early, and inviting transparent communication can keep drama at bay. Curating intentional gatherings, leading with the village values, and sharing the vision openly are ways to attract aligned people while easing fears about judgment.

Part 4 · Defining the Rhythm and Schedule

What does your schedule look like right now?

Days start around 5–6 a.m. with the hope of quiet time before the toddler wakes. Breakfast happens together, followed by outside play, naps around 10 or 11, and support from helpers in the afternoon when available. Some days include YMCA visits, park adventures, or toddler programs. Saturdays focus on music and DJ work while freelance projects weave through the week. Ecstatic dance gatherings in Denver offer community touch points.

What about the current rhythm feels good?

Flexibility.

What would you like to change?

Clarity is still unfolding—listening for what wants to shift.

What aspects of the village vision do you want to weave in next?

Regular sober daytime dance parties, family-friendly celebrations, shared meals with neighbors, and collaborative project days where people help bring spaces—like the dance barn and food forest—to life. Consistent playgroups and intergenerational learning are ready to be woven into the weekly rhythm.

What village elements would you like to weave into the schedule?

Still discovering; experimentation will reveal which pieces—gatherings, projects, shared childcare—fit naturally into the family rhythm.

What support is needed?

Extra hands to clear and outfit shared spaces, childcare swaps that create breathing room, mentors around agreements and governance, and action plans that translate vision into weekly rituals.

What action steps can you take right now?

  • Video call dad.
  • Get a playgroup going—or join one already forming.
  • Clear the barn to activate the dance space (seek support).
  • Call grandparents and reconnect consistently.
  • Tour a nearby inspiring foundation for ideas.
  • Transform partially finished areas into livable spaces.

What is one experiment you could try tomorrow?

Announce and host an interest list for a playgroup in the dance space to build momentum and motivation.
